## Overview

Corporate science — research directed, funded, or controlled by private firms — now drives innovation across pharmaceuticals, artificial intelligence, agriculture, and consumer goods. Yet private R&D prioritizes commercially viable outcomes, frequently marginalizing public interests, ignoring diverse stakeholder perspectives, and concentrating epistemic power among affluent, tech-savvy users whose feedback is most legible to algorithmic systems.

This paper, produced for PHIL 550 (Philosophy of Science) under Prof. Gürol Irzık at Sabanci University, proposes a reframing: **CRM systems** — the digital infrastructures used by virtually every large firm to collect and analyze customer feedback — can be redesigned not merely as marketing tools but as **epistemic interfaces** that democratize corporate science.

## Theoretical Framework

Three philosophical frameworks converge in this argument:

### 1. Well-Ordered Science (Kitcher, 2001)
Philip Kitcher's model proposes that research priorities should emerge from deliberation among informed citizens, not be dictated by market incentives or expert elites alone. Applied to corporate R&D, this requires mechanisms for genuine stakeholder participation in shaping what questions firms pursue.

### 2. Hybrid Forums (Callon, Lascoumes & Barthe, 2009)
Hybrid forums are deliberative spaces where experts and non-experts jointly contribute to knowledge production and policy formation — sites where technical and lay knowledge are treated as equally valid inputs to decision-making. CRM platforms, if redesigned with deliberative principles, could function as scaled digital hybrid forums.

### 3. Epistemic Justice (Fricker, 2007)
Miranda Fricker distinguishes two forms of epistemic injustice: **testimonial injustice** (dismissing individuals as credible sources due to identity-based bias) and **hermeneutical injustice** (lacking shared interpretive resources to understand one's own experience). Current CRM systems perpetuate both: algorithmic filtering privileges certain user voices; opaque processing excludes users from understanding how their input shapes decisions.

## The Core Argument

Current CRM systems are optimized for commercial efficiency, not civic engagement:
- Customer feedback is routed to marketing teams, not R&D or sustainability functions
- Algorithms filter and weight input by commercial relevance, amplifying already-privileged voices
- Users receive no transparency about how their data influences outcomes
- Platform capitalism structures create incentives to extract behavioral data rather than enable deliberation

The paper argues these systems can be redesigned through:
1. **Cross-functional feedback routing** — connecting customer input directly to R&D and product development
2. **Transparent algorithmic governance** — disclosing how feedback is weighted and acted upon
3. **Structured deliberative mechanisms** — moving from passive rating to active dialogue
4. **Inclusive sampling** — counteracting the digital divide that concentrates input among tech-savvy users

## Method

The paper advocates qualitative methods — structured interviews across diverse stakeholder groups (end-users, customer service representatives, R&D teams, executives) and comparative case studies of CRM platforms — over standard survey approaches, arguing that the complex socio-technical dynamics of epistemic inclusion cannot be captured by Likert-scale instruments.

## Significance

As AI systems increasingly mediate the interface between firms and customers, the epistemic architecture of CRM becomes a governance question, not merely a technical one. Reconfiguring these systems as deliberative platforms would not only improve R&D alignment with public values but address structural inequities in whose knowledge shapes technological futures.
