## Overview

Household waste constitutes a major and widely-scattered sustainability challenge. Global food waste alone is estimated at 93 kg per capita per year, and the effectiveness of policies to reduce it depends critically on how they are framed: as potential losses (fines for exceeding a threshold) or as potential gains (subsidies for staying below it).

**Prospect Theory** (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979) predicts an asymmetry: people are more sensitive to losses than to equivalent gains. Applied to waste management, this implies that a fine should reduce waste more effectively than a subsidy of equal monetary magnitude — because the pain of paying a fine registers more strongly than the pleasure of receiving a subsidy.

This project, produced for BAN 502 (Judgment and Decision Making) under Dr. Ayşe Kocabıyıkoğlu, tests this prediction experimentally.

## Experimental Design

A **between-subject design** with three groups of 10 participants each (pilot scale; production study requires 30 per group):

| Group | Condition | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Loss (Group 1)** | Fine | Participants informed they will be fined for waste above the Turkish national average (2.5 kg/10 days) |
| **Gain (Group 2)** | Subsidy | Participants informed they will receive a subsidy for waste below average |
| **Control (Group 3)** | None | No incentive or disincentive; baseline for comparison |

Participants recorded their household waste daily for **10 days**. Pre- and post-study surveys assessed waste management habits and awareness. No deception was used; fines and subsidies were real financial stakes.

## Results

Average waste generation per group over the 10-day period:

| Group | Avg. Waste (kg/10 days) | Change vs. Baseline (2.5 kg) |
|---|---|---|
| Loss (fine) | 1.8 kg | **−28%** |
| Gain (subsidy) | 2.3 kg | −8% |
| Control | 2.6 kg | +4% |

The Loss group achieved the most substantial reduction — a 28% decrease relative to the national baseline. The Gain group showed a modest 8% reduction, and the Control group increased slightly (+4%), likely due to small-sample variability over the short observation window.

## Interpretation

The results are consistent with Prospect Theory's **loss aversion** prediction: the prospect of losing money to a fine produced stronger behavioral change than the prospect of gaining an equivalent subsidy. The financial and psychological weight of a penalty appears to function as a more compelling behavioral lever.

A notable secondary finding: higher-income participants showed greater waste reduction across all groups. This may reflect both greater environmental awareness (linked to education) and a stronger aversion to financial penalties relative to their discretionary spending.

## Policy Implication

For policymakers designing household waste reduction programs, the experiment suggests that penalty-based instruments (fines, per-bag charges, pay-as-you-throw systems) should outperform subsidy-based equivalents in reducing waste volume — at least in the short term. The Prospect Theory framing provides both a theoretical justification and a practical design principle for more effective waste policy.
