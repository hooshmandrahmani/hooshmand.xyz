## Overview

This two-part project, carried out for OPIM 623 (Supply Chain Strategy and Management) at Sabanci University, applies rigorous quantitative methods to two related questions in supply chain management — first, how top-management recognition of purchasing drives supplier integration and performance, and second, how proactive environmental strategies mediate the link between organizational commitment and environmental investment outcomes.

Both studies use survey data collected from manufacturing firms in Turkey (food & beverages, chemicals, and machinery sectors).

---

## Part 1 — Purchasing Recognition, Supplier Integration, and Performance

### Research Question
How does top management's recognition of the purchasing function influence supplier integration and, ultimately, purchasing performance?

### Design
The study evaluates two competing structural configurations:
- **Moderation model**: Long-term commitment moderates the relationship between purchasing recognition and supplier integration
- **Mediation model**: Supplier integration mediates the relationship between purchasing recognition / long-term commitment and purchasing performance

Four constructs, each measured with 5 Likert-scale items:
- **Purchasing Recognition (R)**: extent to which top management values and supports the purchasing department
- **Long-term Commitment (L)**: firm's commitment to sustained supplier relationships
- **Supplier Integration (I)**: information sharing, joint product design, joint process improvement
- **Purchasing Performance (P)**: cost, quality, delivery, and innovation outcomes

### Methods
- **Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA)** with maximum likelihood extraction and varimax rotation
- Bartlett's test of sphericity, KMO values, parallel analysis
- Cronbach's Alpha reliability assessment: R = 0.93, L = 0.89, I = 0.91
- Moderation and mediation testing

### Key Finding
EFA revealed that Supplier Integration items cross-loaded on both Long-term Commitment and Purchasing Performance factors — reflecting genuine conceptual overlap: supplier integration is both an expression of long-term commitment and a driver of performance. The purchasing recognition construct loaded cleanly and independently, confirming its distinct role in the model.

## Part 2 — Proactive Environmental Strategies and Environmental Performance

### Research Question
How do customer pressure and organizational commitment drive firms toward proactive environmental strategies, and how do these strategies translate into environmental performance through internal and external investments?

### Design
A revised structural model (based on Ateş et al. 2012) tests 11 hypotheses across five constructs:
- **Customer Pressure**: environmental compliance demands and waste reduction requests from customers
- **Organizational Commitment**: management support and cross-functional coordination for environmental initiatives
- **Proactive Environmental Strategies**: beyond-compliance practices, supplier audits
- **Internal Environmental Investments**: eco-design, design for disassembly, reuse, and recycling
- **External Environmental Investments**: cooperation with suppliers for eco-design
- **Environmental Performance**: reductions in emissions, material use, and energy

### Method: PLS-SEM (SmartPLS 4)
Analysis followed a two-step approach: (1) measurement model assessment and (2) structural model evaluation with bootstrapping (500 subsamples, two-tailed, 0.05 significance).

**Measurement model results:**
- All item loadings > 0.70 (lowest: 0.74 for "reduction of material use")
- Composite Reliability: 0.92–0.96 across all constructs
- Cronbach's Alpha: 0.89–0.95
- Organizational Commitment items showed the highest loadings (all > 0.90)

### Key Finding
Proactive environmental strategies fully mediated the effect of both customer pressure and organizational commitment on environmental investment behaviors — firms that adopted beyond-compliance practices systematically invested more in eco-design and supplier collaboration, which in turn significantly improved environmental performance. Direct effects of customer pressure and commitment on investment were weaker, consistent with a strategy-mediated pathway.
