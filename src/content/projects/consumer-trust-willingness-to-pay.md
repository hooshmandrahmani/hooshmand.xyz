## Overview

**Agrivoltaics** integrates solar energy generation with agricultural production on the same land — solar panels are installed so that crops can grow beneath them or animals graze around them. Scientific studies show that partial shading creates a microclimate that conserves water and can improve yields for certain crops (lettuce, potatoes, tomatoes). For farmers, agrivoltaics offers both crop income and electricity revenue.

Despite these advantages, agrivoltaic systems require significant upfront investment. Consumer willingness to pay a premium for agrivoltaic produce is therefore crucial for financial viability — yet this question has barely been studied. Existing work on sustainable consumer behavior focuses on generic organic goods; the distinctive characteristics of agrivoltaics (novel technology, uncertain food-safety perceptions, dual-purpose land use) remain unexamined.

This working paper, developed under Dr. Melek Akın Ateş at Sabanci University, addresses that gap.

## Research Question

> How does trust influence the relationship between sustainability attributes and consumers' willingness to pay in agrivoltaic systems?

## Theoretical Framework

The model tests three hypotheses grounded in the behavioral literature:

- **H1**: Green trust positively correlates with willingness to pay (WTP) for agrivoltaic products. Trust compensates for the consumer's inability to directly verify sustainability claims.
- **H2**: Climate change risk awareness positively correlates with WTP. Consumers more aware of climate threats see the premium as a worthwhile sustainability investment.
- **H3**: Climate change risk awareness positively affects green trust. Environmentally concerned consumers are more receptive to novel climate-adaptation technologies.

Together, H1–H3 test whether **green trust mediates** the positive path from climate risk awareness to WTP — a mechanism not previously examined in the agrivoltaics context.

## Research Design

- **Method**: Cross-sectional survey with Structural Equation Modeling (PLS-SEM via SmartPLS)
- **Sample**: Target of 1,600 respondents from Turkey, stratified across the seven geographical regions (Marmara, Aegean, Mediterranean, Black Sea, Central Anatolia, Eastern Anatolia, Southeastern Anatolia) with balance across age, gender, education, and income
- **Survey instrument**: Climate Risk Awareness scale (van der Linden 2015 / Leiserowitz 2006, 8 items); Green Trust scale (Chen 2010, 5 items); WTP measured as a binary + ordinal response (maximum acceptable price premium in five bands)
- **Control variables**: age, gender, education, income, geographic region

A survey introduction includes a factual description of agrivoltaics with images to ensure respondents understand the technology before answering.

## Measurement Model

Reliability and validity of all multi-item scales are assessed via:
- **Cronbach's Alpha** and **Composite Reliability** (threshold: > 0.70)
- **Convergent validity**: Average Variance Extracted (AVE > 0.50)
- **Discriminant validity**: HTMT criterion
